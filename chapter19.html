Chapter 19

The Difference Between Certainty and Trust

One of the most persistent assumptions people carry about fear is the belief that fear would disappear if only they possessed enough certainty.

At first glance, the idea seems reasonable. After all, uncertainty has appeared repeatedly throughout this book as one of fear's favorite environments. Fear often grows where answers are incomplete, where outcomes remain unknown, and where the future refuses to reveal itself ahead of schedule. It is understandable, therefore, that many people conclude certainty must be the solution.

Yet life presents a complication.

Certainty is remarkably difficult to obtain.

Even in situations where answers seem clear, new questions eventually emerge. Every season of life contains variables that cannot be controlled, outcomes that cannot be guaranteed, and possibilities that remain beyond prediction. The more honestly we examine life, the more we discover that complete certainty has never truly been available.

This realization creates an important question.

If certainty is unavailable, how do people move forward?

How do they make decisions?

How do they build relationships?

How do they pursue opportunities?

How do they embrace the future without becoming consumed by fear?

The answer may be found in something entirely different from certainty.

It may be found in trust.

Although certainty and trust are often confused with one another, they are not the same thing. In fact, they operate in very different ways.

Certainty depends upon having complete knowledge.

Trust functions despite incomplete knowledge.

Certainty requires answers before movement.

Trust allows movement before every answer arrives.

Certainty seeks guarantees.

Trust accepts that guarantees may never come.

This distinction explains why fear remains so persistent in the lives of many people. Fear continually directs attention toward certainty, encouraging the belief that peace can only exist once every uncertainty has been resolved. Since that level of certainty never arrives, peace remains permanently postponed.

The cycle becomes endless.

One question receives an answer, only to be replaced by another. One concern is resolved, only to reveal a new uncertainty waiting nearby. The search for certainty continues because fear insists that the next answer will finally provide the security we seek.

Yet security built entirely upon certainty is fragile because certainty itself is fragile.

Circumstances change.

People change.

Plans change.

Life changes.

Anything dependent upon complete predictability becomes vulnerable the moment unpredictability enters the picture.

Trust operates differently.

Trust does not require perfect knowledge of the future. Instead, trust acknowledges the limitations of what can be known and chooses to move forward anyway. It accepts that uncertainty is part of life while refusing to allow uncertainty to become the sole author of the future.

This does not mean trust is easy.

Trust often feels risky precisely because it requires stepping forward without possessing all the information we would prefer to have. There is vulnerability in trust. There is humility in admitting that not every outcome can be controlled or predicted. There is courage in continuing the journey despite unanswered questions.

Fear struggles to understand this perspective because fear equates vulnerability with danger.

Trust recognizes that vulnerability is often the doorway through which life is fully experienced.

Every meaningful relationship involves trust.

Every worthwhile opportunity involves trust.

Every act of courage involves trust.

Every significant step into the future involves trust.

No one waits until certainty arrives before living.

Life would never begin if they did.

The more closely we examine fear, the more we discover that many fears are ultimately attempts to avoid vulnerability. Fear seeks guarantees because guarantees appear safer than trust. Fear prefers prediction because prediction feels more controllable than trust. Fear wants certainty because certainty appears to remove risk.

The problem is that certainty rarely delivers what fear promises.

Even when certainty seems present, life has a way of reminding us that complete control was never ours to begin with. New circumstances emerge. Unexpected developments occur. The future continues unfolding according to realities larger than our ability to manage.

Trust acknowledges this reality instead of fighting it.

There is a quiet strength in that acknowledgment.

Trust does not claim to know everything.

Trust does not pretend uncertainty has disappeared.

Trust simply refuses to surrender peace every time a question remains unanswered.

This may be one of the most important turning points in understanding fear before fear.

Much of fear's development depends upon the assumption that uncertainty is intolerable. Fear treats uncertainty as a problem that must be solved before life can be fully lived. Trust offers a different perspective. It suggests that uncertainty may never be completely removed and that life can still be embraced despite its presence.

That shift changes everything.

The future remains unknown, yet it no longer appears quite so threatening.

Questions remain unanswered, yet they no longer dominate every thought.

Possibilities continue existing, yet they no longer carry the same power to create fear.

Not because certainty has finally arrived, but because trust has begun occupying the space certainty once demanded.

This is where fear often begins losing its deepest hold.

Fear wants us to believe that security is found in knowing what comes next.

Experience repeatedly teaches a different lesson.

Security is often found in learning how to move forward even when we do not.

As our journey draws closer to its conclusion, the distinction between certainty and trust becomes increasingly significant. We have spent many chapters exploring how fear develops through stories, expectations, assumptions, uncertainty, and perception. We have examined the ways fear gains influence long before it is consciously recognized.

Now we are beginning to see how that influence weakens.

Not through greater control.

Not through perfect prediction.

Not through complete certainty.

But through a different relationship with the unknown.

And that relationship may hold one of the most powerful answers fear has been trying to prevent us from discovering all along.
